COLA Glossary

Understanding the terminology used in TTB Certificate of Label Approval records. This information is sourced from the Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB).

Jump to Section

The Basics Product Information Company Information Wine-Specific Fields Application Types Status Definitions Intelligence Signals

The Basics

COLA

Certificate of Label Approval. This is the official authorization from the TTB that allows an alcohol beverage to be sold in the United States. Every wine, spirit, and malt beverage sold in interstate commerce must have an approved COLA before it can be bottled (domestic) or released from customs (imported).

TTB ID

A unique 14-digit number assigned by TTB to track each COLA application. The number contains encoded information:

  • First 5 digits: Calendar year and Julian date the application was received
  • Digits 6-8: How it was received (001 = electronic, 002/003 = mailed, 000 = hand delivered)
  • Last 6 digits: Sequential number for that day

Example: 25001001000123 = Received January 1, 2025, filed electronically, 123rd application that day

Approval Date

The date when TTB officially approved the COLA application. This is when the label becomes legal to use.

Product Information

Brand Name

The name under which a product is sold. If the product isn't sold under a specific brand name, the bottler, packer, or importer's name becomes the brand name.

Example: "Jack Daniel's", "Tito's", "Kendall-Jackson"

Fanciful Name

An additional name used alongside the brand name to further identify a specific product. Required for malt beverage and distilled spirit specialty products; optional for others.

Example: "Single Barrel Select" (for Jack Daniel's), "Handmade" (for Tito's)

Class/Type (Subcategory)

The official classification of the product according to TTB regulations. Each product is assigned a unique class/type code that determines labeling requirements.

Examples: "Straight Bourbon Whisky", "Vodka", "Red Table Wine", "India Pale Ale"

Origin

The country (for imports) or state (for domestic products) where the product was produced. Each location has a unique origin code.

Alcohol Content

The percentage of alcohol by volume. Required on all wine and distilled spirits labels. Optional for malt beverages, but if shown, must follow the correct format.

Net Contents

The volume of liquid in the container. Must appear on the label or be molded into the container itself. Distilled spirits and wine must use standard container sizes (750ml, 1L, etc.). Malt beverages have no size restrictions.

Formula

A quantitative list of ingredients and step-by-step manufacturing method. Required for certain products (flavored spirits, specialty wines, etc.) and must be approved by TTB before the COLA can be issued.

Serial Number

A unique number assigned by the COLA holder (not TTB). The first two digits represent the calendar year; the remaining characters (up to 4) are assigned by the company to track their own applications.

Company Information

Plant Registry / Basic Permit / Brewer's Number

A unique identifier assigned by TTB to each business location authorized to produce or import alcohol beverages.

Examples: BW-NY-123 (Bonded Winery), DSP-KY-89 (Distilled Spirits Plant), BR-WI-ABC-567 (Brewery)

Permit Name

The official operating name and/or owner name associated with a Plant Registry, Basic Permit, or Brewer's Number.

Vendor Code

A legacy code previously used to identify organizations submitting COLAs. No longer actively used, but appears in historical records.

Wine-Specific Fields

Grape Varietal

The grape variety used to make the wine (e.g., Cabernet Sauvignon, Chardonnay). If shown on the label, at least 75% of the wine must come from that grape variety (85% in Oregon).

Appellation

The geographic origin of the grapes used to make the wine. Can be a country, state, county, or approved viticultural area (AVA). Requirements vary:

  • Country/State/County: At least 75% of grapes must be from the named area
  • Viticultural Area (AVA): At least 85% of grapes must be from the named AVA

Examples: "Napa Valley", "Sonoma Coast", "Willamette Valley"

Vintage

The year the grapes were harvested. If shown on the label, at least 95% of the wine must come from grapes harvested that year, and the wine must have an appellation smaller than a country.

pH Level

A measure of acidity in the wine. Lower pH means higher acidity. Typically ranges from 3.0 to 4.0 for most wines.

Application Types

Certificate of Label Approval (COLA)

The standard approval that authorizes a product to be sold in interstate commerce (across state lines). Required for all products sold outside the state where they're produced.

Certificate of Exemption

An alternative to a full COLA that allows a product to be sold only within the state where it was bottled. Products must be labeled "For sale in [state] only." Not available for malt beverages or imported products.

Distinctive Liquor Bottle

A spirits container where the contents aren't visible through the container, or the shape/design is non-standard. Requires additional photos and approval of the bottle itself, along with the label.

Status Definitions

Approved

The COLA application has been reviewed and accepted. The label is legal to use.

Received

TTB has received the application but hasn't completed review yet.

Needs Correction

TTB found issues with the application that must be fixed before approval. The applicant has a limited time to make corrections before automatic rejection.

Rejected

The application was denied. This can happen if required corrections weren't made in time, or if the label violates regulations.

Withdrawn

The applicant voluntarily cancelled their application before it was approved or rejected.

Surrendered

The COLA holder voluntarily gave up their approval after it was granted. The label can no longer be used.

Revoked

TTB cancelled a previously approved COLA, typically due to regulatory violations discovered after approval.

Intelligence Signals

BevAlc Intelligence analyzes every COLA filing to identify market signals. These classifications help you spot new market entrants, brand launches, and competitive activity.

NEW COMPANY

A company appearing in our database for the first time. This can signal a new market entrant - a startup, a company expanding into beverage alcohol, or a new importer. However, it could also be an established company filing for the first time, one that changed their legal name, or one using a different filing entity.

Example: A craft distillery submitting their first whiskey label after receiving their DSP permit.

NEW BRAND

An established company launching a new brand. This indicates product line expansion, market repositioning, or response to consumer trends. Watch for patterns across categories.

Example: A vodka company launching a new tequila brand to enter a growing category.

NEW SKU

A new product variant under an existing brand - new flavor, size, age statement, or formulation. This shows active product development and brand investment.

Example: A bourbon brand adding a 12-year age statement to their existing lineup.

REFILE

A label that has been filed before, now being resubmitted. Common reasons include label design updates, regulatory corrections, or preparing for a new production run. High refile counts indicate established, actively-sold products.

Example: A popular wine updating their vintage year from 2023 to 2024.

Pro Tip: Filter by "NEW COMPANY" and "NEW BRAND" signals to focus on genuine market activity and filter out routine refiles. This is available to Pro subscribers in the database.
